In Brown v.s the board of education, a father, Oliver Brown, sued the Board of Education-Topeka, Kansas after his daughter was denied entry into an all-white school. The supreme court ruled that separating children in public schools on the basis of race was unconstitutional and ended the legalization of school segregation. This monumental case was the beginning of the era in the school district. However, even with the law passed, many policies have been in place in order to continue the segregation of schools under the law, such as redlining. Redlining was the practice of refusing to give funding to certain neighborhoods, usually Black communities. The cause and effects of redlining greatly affect the present-day public school system. Because African Americans were pushed to poorer underdeveloped neighborhoods and were unable to get loans, the value of education and neighborhood went significantly down. No one was building nice grocery stores or beautiful buildings in those neighborhoods because no one invested in those neighborhoods. There was more business in white communities because those weren't redlined. Moreover School policy forced children to go to school where their neighborhood was. Less funding made for inedquate building and education. There was just no money for it due to the property value being low. Affulent neighborhoods were where the good schools were and it wasn't a coincidence that those were where white families were located as well. This history of redlining has contributed to the segregation of schools today.
